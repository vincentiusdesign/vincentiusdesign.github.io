Conceptual Design Book

"Instructions"

Instructions is a book about rules â€” a compendium of mundane life-hacks, sacred principles, governmental commands, and homespun hints. By juxtaposing these incongruent viewpoints I hope to collapse any sense of objectivity, to recognize the ways in which our freedoms are constrained through insidious systems of power.

My aim is to show that those who preach moral instruction are just as flawed and self-interested as the rest of us.

The style of this book references a kind of drab bureaucratic Modernism.

It is designed with Helvetica, bullet-pointed notes, and linear diagrams.

The strait-laced layouts are then disrupted through discordant color schemes and strangely abstracted imagery.

These graphic interventions are meant to represent a rupture of the normal, and to displace the rigid ideologies of our time.

Total Power Move Lecture Slides

"Toxic Masculinity"

Slides for a lecture about toxic masculinity. A high-contrast color palette is utilized to visually echo the lecture's divisive themes. The saturated orange background was selected to be polarizing, intentionally provoking strong reactions.

The blue drop shadow was specifically chosen because it serves a dual function: its complementary nature opposes the orange, while the color itself has widespread popular appeal. This deliberate combination of jarring and approachable colors mirrors the complex and conflicting nature of the subject matter.

Agipo Regular Extended serves as the large type and heading font, while Agipo Regular is reserved for smaller body text.

Though they share the same weight, the marginally wider letterforms of the Extended version create a discreet hierarchy that enhances legibility.

The value of the background progressively gets darker with each slide, conjuring a feeling of impending doom.

The curved typography is suggestively phallic in form.

The motion indicates flaccidity, emphasizing the "toxic" aspect of masculinity in the story being told.

As the intense saturation fades and the background changes, quotes from the father, which are in an agreeable blue hue, become easier to read. Reflecting the sentiment of the lecturer toward his father's changing outlook.

Much of the type in these slides is intentionally off center and the ragging is inconsistent. I set the margins and flushed everything to the left except the lists and small paragraphs. Like the speaker's father I wanted the type to be stubborn, obstinate, and set in its ways without any compensation or consideration.

Once the background reaches pure black, at the climax of the lecture, it then fades to white.

The white background corresponds with talk of the father's death. As the father seemed to reflect and realize his mistakes near the end of his life, his quotes are also most legible near the end of the slides.

Sans Serif Logotype

"Snackies"

For the owner of the Mobil Gas Station in New York City located on 13th street and 8th avenue. The client wanted a logo to brand his own line of snacks and products.

After doing research on corporate food logos and colorways I began working with Franklin Gothic Demi as a base font. Though it is mostly used in publications and corporate logos, there is a subtle playfulness that I wanted emphasize through the change in context and use of color.

Realizing the terminals are sheared (angled) I altered them to be horizontal to give the letterforms consistency because I intended to warp the type later in the project to add the dynamism found in most corporate food logos.

Modifying the font allowed me to analyze the nuances of the letters in great detail. The aperture of the capital and lowercase 's do not match the width or shape of the eye of the e, the counter in the a, or the aperture of any of the other letters. I'm sure this difference was accounted for in the design, particularly when it comes to the overall visual balance in large bodies of text. However, when it comes to the small sample size in this logo it is very noticeable.

I found that the 's in Agipo Condensed Bold resemble the forms I was looking for, but the apertures are tapered slightly and the terminals are flared out compared to the monowidth strokes of Franklin Gothic.

Through many iterations the modified Agipo Condensed Bold 's that I ended up with better serve this logos needs than the Franklin Gothic 's that I started with. This project was an excellent learning experience that deepened my understanding of type design.
